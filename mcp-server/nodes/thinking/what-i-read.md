Reading is the main thing I do outside work. Three categories, each for different reasons.

## Urdu poetry — the density lens

Jaun Elia, Mir Taqi Mir, Allama Iqbal, Ahmad Faraz. The ghazal form specifically, because of how much one couplet can hold. Philosophy, love, grief, rebellion — sometimes all at once in two lines.

Reading Urdu poetry trains a particular discipline: saying something exact in compressed form. The opposite of most technical writing, which sprawls. Every time I come back to it I end up writing shorter commits, tighter PR descriptions, cleaner system prompts. The transfer is real.

Sufi philosophy comes in through that door. Not as a separate study — as the undercurrent of a lot of the poetry itself. Questions about what the self is, what's worth attaching to, what's real versus performed. Useful counter-weight to the startup ecosystem's relentless performativity.

## Fiction — world-builders

Frank Herbert's *Dune*. George R.R. Martin's *A Song of Ice and Fire*. The kind of fiction where the world is a character, not just a setting. Political economy, factions, how institutions fail, what power actually looks like when it's not being simulated.

Both are also studies in long-term thinking — Herbert writing thousands of years of imagined history, Martin tracking a decade+ of consequences inside a single plot. The intellectual experience of holding a system that complex in your head, then watching it resolve, is rare. Most modern storytelling has ADHD by comparison.

## Non-fiction — the useful canon

The books I've actually read, no padding:

- **The Psychology of Money** (Morgan Housel) — behavioral finance explained without the condescension finance usually carries.
- **Man's Search for Meaning** (Viktor Frankl) — the book I reach for when I need to recalibrate what problems are actually problems.
- **The Courage to Be Disliked** (Kishimi & Koga) — Adlerian psychology in dialogue form. Useful if you're someone who over-negotiates with other people's opinions.
- **The Burnout Society** (Byung-Chul Han) — short, uncomfortable diagnosis of performance culture. The opposite of a self-help book; closer to philosophy.
- **How to Win Friends and Influence People** (Dale Carnegie) — cliché recommendation, still holds up if you treat it as observations not tactics.
- **Ikigai** — the concept is worth the ~2 hours the book takes.
- **The Alchemist** (Paulo Coelho) — the fable about listening to yourself.
- **The Monk Who Sold His Ferrari** (Robin Sharma) — the same fable dressed differently.
- **Eat That Frog** (Brian Tracy) — a one-line idea stretched into a book; the one line is worth it.

## Why this list matters

You can read an engineer's code and get part of their mind. You can read what they've built and get another part. The books they choose to reread tell you the shape of the thinking behind both — the priors that show up in every decision they make without announcing themselves.

This isn't a recommendation list. These are just the ones I've read and gotten something out of. The Urdu poetry list is the part I'd defend most strongly, and is the least legible to most tech readers.
