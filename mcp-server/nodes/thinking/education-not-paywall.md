Simple position: **education shouldn't be gatekept behind a paywall. Not just hardware — all of it.**

Knowledge is cheap to replicate. The internet proved that. Artificial scarcity around something that costs almost nothing to distribute is a choice, not a property of the thing.

That position shows up in the work:

**The IoT courses on SkillAnything are free.** Two tracks — IoT for Juniors (22 lessons, the Arduino-basics intro) and IoT Beginner to Expert (70 lessons, the deeper track covering ESP8266/ESP32, AWS IoT, Python, Raspberry Pi, ML-for-IoT basics). Both free. If someone in a small town wants to learn embedded systems, the hardware kit is already a barrier. The course shouldn't be another one.

**This portfolio is open-source.** The entire code lives on GitHub under MIT. Fork it, swap the markdown nodes for your own writing, and you have your own personal-brand AI agent. The point isn't to keep the implementation private — the point is to make it copy-paste-able so other builders can do the same thing faster than I did.

**The AI agent you're talking to right now is open-source.** The MCP server, the router, the responder, the safety middleware — all of it. The "how it works" is the teaching artifact. The README at `mcp-server/` is the 30-minute fork walkthrough.

## Why I'm direct about this

There's a perfectly reasonable version of the same technical work that sits behind a paid course or a gated Discord or a Substack paywall. I'm not against those models for everyone — but they're not my model.

The reason: the teachers who shaped me weren't paywalled. YouTube tutorials, blog posts, open-source repos, Stack Overflow answers. People putting work into the world without asking for money back. The right way to repay that is to do the same thing for whoever comes next.

**Knowledge you can't pay for is the best kind.** The internet already made that true in principle. The practical version happens whenever someone who knows something decides to put it on the internet for free.

This also just flows from how I think about the craft: if an engineer's edge in 2026 is judgment, taste, and ownership (see the coders-to-owners thesis), then the tutorial-level content is the LEAST valuable part of what they know. It makes no sense to paywall the least valuable part.

## The practical version

For builders who want to do the same thing:

- Open-source the code that's actually re-usable
- Don't open-source things that shouldn't be copied (unedited private writing, credentials, anything with a legal gotcha)
- Write docs that teach, not docs that impress
- Accept that the best response to people finding your work is more people finding your work

That's the whole philosophy. Ship, document, share. Over and over.
